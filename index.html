Section 1

The highway analogy — who's who

Think of BBPS as a national highway system. Money is the cargo, and it needs to travel from a customer in one city to a biller (electricity company, FASTag provider, gas board) in another. Four parties make this work:

NPCI

Highway Authority

Sets the rules, maintains the road, operates the central clearing house. Every transaction passes through here.

Axis Bank

Licensed Fleet

The only one legally allowed to move money. Holds the BBPS license, settlement account, and compliance responsibility.

Setu

GPS & Engine

Technology that makes the car move. Provides APIs, encryption, routing logic, and biller integration. Cannot operate without Axis Bank.

Biller

Destination Factory

The electricity board, gas provider, FASTag issuer. Receives the payment. Plugs in via the BOU layer.

A TSP (Technology Service Provider) like Setu cannot connect to NPCI independently — it must be anchored to a Sponsor Bank. The bank provides the legal and financial validity that Setu alone cannot.

Section 2

The COU hierarchy — OU, AI, and Agents

The customer-side of BBPS (COU — Customer Operating Unit) has a strict four-level hierarchy. Think of it like a university system:

OU — Operating Unit

Axis Bank

Holds settlement funds. Owns the NPCI license. Compliance and risk. Does NOT perform transactions directly.

AI — Agent Institution

Setu

Technology aggregator. Manages agents, float balances, routes payments. At least one AI must exist per OU.

Agent IDs are structured: AS01-A02-MOB-005 — AS01 = Axis Bank (OU), A02 = Agent Institution, MOB = Mobile channel, 005 = agent number. This ID travels with every transaction.

All white label customers sit under a single AI node (e.g. AS03). NPCI validates the UI once — every new white label agent inherits that approval. Faster onboarding, lower cost, central control.

Section 4

The Biller side — BOU explained

Everything so far has been the customer side. The Biller Operating Unit (BOU) is the entrance ramp for billers — how an electricity board or gas provider plugs in to BBPS. Setu acts as the BOU in your environment.

BOU role

Setu (as BOU)

Provides the API layer, encryption, and integration logic between the biller's backend and the BBPS network.

Biller's job

Validate & Acknowledge

Confirm the customer ID exists, return bill details, and acknowledge that a payment has been received.

Onboarding a biller (AOI flow — Agent Onboarding Integration):

1
Agreement — Biller signs with Axis Bank. Legal and commercial terms established.
2
Tech sync — Setu connects the biller's backend to the BBPS APIs. Integration and testing.
3
Certification — NPCI approves and certifies the integration. Biller goes live.

The Action. Initiates the actual money transfer. Golden rule: the entire cycle must complete within 180 seconds. Setu manages the biller acknowledgement window — up to 120 seconds of that.

POST /bbps/bills/status CTS

Check Transaction Status — the safety net. Used when a biller's server times out. Setu asks "did that payment eventually land?" instead of leaving the transaction in limbo. Used every time in the Deemed model for final ground truth.

Section 8

The 5 golden rules

Every operational decision in the Setu + Axis Bank environment flows from these five rules. If something isn't making sense, come back here.

1
Only agents can transact. OU and AI are infrastructure, not transactors. A transaction must originate from an agent ID — never from Axis Bank or Setu directly.
2
OU holds funds, AI manages agents. Axis Bank is always the settlement layer. Setu manages the agents below it and their float balances.
3
Money flows upward on payment. Customer → Agent → AI → OU → NPCI → Biller. Always in this direction, no exceptions.
4
Internal fund transfer happens before NPCI. Agent balance is checked and money moved to the float account first. If balance fails, NPCI never sees the transaction.
5
Refunds flow downward, in exact reverse. NPCI → OU → AI → Agent → Customer. The chain works the same way in both directions.
Memorise rule 4. It's the most operationally important and the least obvious. The internal fund transfer is BBPS's built-in risk control — no balance, no exposure to the network.
